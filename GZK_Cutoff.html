Cosmic Rays

  • 89% protons, 10% helium nuclei, 1% heavier nuclei
  • ~1 muon per second per human head
  • Energy ranges from 1 GeV to $10^8$ TeV
- Discovered in 1912 by Victor Hess on a balloon - 89% protons, 10% helium nuclei, 1% heavier nuclei - Collide with nuclei in upper atmoshpere, create mainly pions - Pions decay quickly into muons, which travel to earth - CR flux is ~1 muon per second per human head - Energy ranges from 1 GeV to 1e8 TeV

Ultra High Energy Cosmic Rays (UHECRs)

  • Flux falls off with energy, from $10^5/m^2/s$ to $<1 /km^2/$century
  • Produce showers of 10 billion particles, spread over $20km^2$
  • Sources:
    • Remnant supernovae, e.g. Crab Nebula
    • supermassive black holes
    • high-energy astrophysical phenomena
- Flux falls off with energy, from 1e5/m^2/s to <1 /km^2/century. - UHECRs can produce showers of 10 billion particles, spread over 20km^2 - Sources - low energy: solar wind. High energy: hard to tell - Can use high-energy gamma rays (no magnetism) to find sources of charged CRs - UHECRs come from supernova remnants i.e. crab nebula, or SMBHs.

The GZK Cut-Off

$p + \gamma \rightarrow \Delta^+ \rightarrow \rho^+ + \pi^0$ or $n + \pi^+$

Minimum energy: \( p^\mu_p + p^\mu_\gamma = p^\mu_\Delta \)

\( m_p^2 + 2p_p\cdot p_\gamma + 0 = m_\Delta^2 \)

\( p_p\cdot p_\gamma = |p_p||p_\gamma|\cos{\theta} \approx E_p \cdot E_\gamma \cos{\theta} \)

\( E_p = \frac{m_\Delta^2 - m_p^2}{2E_\gamma(1-\cos{\theta})} = 2.4 \times 10^{20} \) eV

- due to collisions of protons with photons of CMB, produce pions - reduces proton energy by ~20%, continues until energy lower threshold reached - only protons below this threshold will reach us - proton + photon -> delta^+ -> rho^+ + pi^0 or neutron + pi^+ - calculate minimum energy such that the proton can just produce a pion - consider the proton + gamma -> delta^+ process. Four-momentum is conserved - p^\mu_p + p^\mu_\gamma = p^\mu_\delta - square this, use p^2 = m^2 - m_p^2 + 2p^\mu\cdotp_\gamma + 0 = m_delta^2 - p^\mu\cdotp_\gamma = \abs{p_p}\abs{p_\gamma}\cos{\theta} \approx E_p*E_\gamma\cos{ \theta} - E_p = \frac{m_\delta^2-m_p^2}{2E_\gamma(1-\cos{\theta}} - experimental data for m_\delta = 1.232e9 eV, m_p = 9.383e8 eV, E_\gamma=6.626e-4 eV - pick \theta = \pi for lowest energy. Add back in c^4 for units - E_p = 2.4e20 eV

The GZK Cut-Off

  • Including CMB thermal distribution and \( \Delta \) resonance makes it slightly lower, \( E_p < 5 \times 10^{19} \) eV \( \approx 8 \)J
  • Very high energy for proton, \( v = 0.9999999999999999999998c \) (21 9s)
  • GZK cutoff experimentally observed in 2008 (controversial, unconfirmed?)

Experimental Verification

The Telescope Array Experiment

- International collaboration - Japan, US, Russia, South Korea, Belgium - Located in Utah, 1400m altitude - Hybrid system designed for CR shower detection - 507 scintillation surface detectors measure charged particle distribution - 3 fluorescence stations for night sky observation, measure scintillation light from charged particles in atmosphere, can measure primary particle mass - LIDAR system for atmospheric monitoring

Key Measurements

- Surface detectors are evenly distributed across a 762km^2 grid, 1.2km between each - Surface detectors have 3m^2 of scintillating detection area - Uses arrival time of shower signal at different detectors to measure direction

Summary
